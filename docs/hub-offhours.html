THE OFF·HOURS

Author the nights that make a veteran someone you'd grieve.

A nightlife district for the STARLEFT H.U.B. — a dive bar, a club, a noodle-bar — where you direct your veterans through scenes with the people the dossier already named, and with each other. No Founder, no avatar: you're the hand, they're the lives. This is the whole build, every system, and the city it leaves behind.

03

After Hours

what the HUB becomes

Debrief's filed. Somewhere over the sprawl the ad-drones reset for the night shift. Down here, the district wakes up. This is the H.U.B. once the Off-Hours ships — not a row of buildings, but a place where the people you'd grieve go to be people. You don't walk in. You send them, and you choose, line by line, what they say. Here's a night.

▍ The Late Shift — dive bar

The bartender keeps a stool

confidant arc · Hades (NPC confidants) × Persona

There's a bartender who has poured for three rosters of company dead. She's a retired pilot — one of the founder-mech survivors who outlived her whole unit — and she is the closest thing the war has to a priest. She is not you. You have no body in this room. There's just Rust, a hustler who sold out his first crew before the company found him, and the woman behind the bar, and the knockoff whiskey she slides him that he didn't order.

You take it slow. First night you let him just sit. A few visits later, with a gift of real coffee on the counter — he's from a place that still had it — you pick the line where he finally says the thing he did. Not to a confessor. To a bartender who's heard worse. The check could misfire; if it does, he deflects onto the war instead, and that's its own truth. Tonight it lands.

"Told someone what I did to the people from home. First time out loud." — and that sentence is now a line in his service record, written by a night at a bar.

Twelve episodes later Rust falls. You open his file at The Wake, and that line is still there, in the same log as his kills and his promotions. You read it before you decide whether the storm gets to keep him.

▍ Marisol's — noodle bar

The brother who took three buses

reunion / heart event · Stardew Valley × Dragon Age

The dossier always said Nino had an estranged brother named Tomas. The city already spawned him — he's been in a condo this whole time, a real resident with his own small life. He just never had a table to sit at. Now he does. You walk Nino in. Tomas is already in the back booth, hands around a bowl he isn't eating.

The first night you pick blunt — Nino stays in the doorway, arms crossed — and Tomas leaves. That's allowed; nobody is punished, and it's recoverable. The second night you pick warm, and Nino says his brother's name before anything else, and the broth gets cold while they talk. A life-event writes to both their files at once. By the third night it's mended.

And on another night, a different booth: the kid sister of a vet who's already on the Wall, mourning, nursing one alone. You can't send her brother — he's gone. But you can send a squadmate who knew him to sit with the family. What you do at that table can decide who the storm reaches for first.

vet ↔ kin NPC · dual-write to dossier + the relative's life-log · grief that was earned

▍ Static — the club

Two soldiers, one table, the bass

squad bonds · XCOM 2: War of the Chosen × RimWorld

No NPC here — just two of your own. You put them at the same sticky table and you direct both of them, turn by turn, because both are yours. Their dossiers decide the weather: shared hometowns and complementary wounds pull them toward each other; a crime one of them would judge pulls them apart. High compatibility, three nights, and a co-deployment, and they're inseparable — one closest bond, the kind a campaign only grants once.

It buys you a little something on the battlefield if you field them together — you don't have to. And then one of them falls, and the survivor doesn't take it like a stat. The grief beat the game already plays hits harder because you built the thing that broke.

You don't get attached to one hero here. You get attached to a dozen veterans — because you're the showrunner, not the lead.

▍ The Wake — the wall it all feeds

Collateral the game can spend on you

the payoff turned inward · everything above

The Off-Hours never asks to be the point. It feeds the one that already is. Every night you author is collateral — a reconciled brother, a confession in a service record, a friendship you watched form — that The Wake can later cash in. The district unlocks before the storm does, on purpose, so the attachments exist to be taken.

That's the whole machine: cold systems — a bond ledger, a scene engine, a deterministic check — wired to make a death land. The build is on the left. This is what it's for.
